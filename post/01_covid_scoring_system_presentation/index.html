Simplifying the French Covid-19 patient orientation algorithm

From a decision tree to a point-based scoring system

 Posted on April 9, 2020  |  Jean-Matthieu Schertzer

The ongoing Covid-19 outbreak has made it essential for authorities to manage how populations in need should contact either general practitioners or emergency medical services. In France, an official orientation algorithm has been released to give recommendations to patients, depending on their symptoms and medical history.

After slightly simplifying this official decision tree, I present an original contribution: a Covid-19 orientation scoring system which is logically equivalent. Being the other type of decision-aid algorithm largely used in medicine, scoring systems offer a better understanding of the relative importance of the different factors. Using Covid-19 orientation illustrations, a comparative analysis is carried out concerning decision trees and scoring systems.

Context and objectives of the blog post

The Covid-19 outbreak in France challenges its healthcare system ability to care and treat patients. In this context, an efficient orientation of potential patients is a pre-requisite to improve reactivity and good use of scarce resources: medical emergency telephone lines and medical emergency vehicles.

In early April 2020 and under population containment, the French strategy has been to promote real-time tele-consultation1 with general practitioners and other physicians when possible, so that emergency services are less overwhelmed. As presented in the next section, an orientation algorithm has been built in France to give personal recommendations to the potential patients, depending on their symptoms and background medical profile and history.

First of all, this blog post gives some additional context to the official orientation algorithm. Secondly, a slightly simplified version of the official decision tree is presented. Thirdly, the simplified decision tree is transformed into a point-based scoring system, the other type of decision-aid algorithm largely used in medicine. Pros and cons of both algorithm types are discussed in a comparative analysis, illustrated by the Covid-19 orientation problem.

The original contribution of this blog post is the weights of the scoring system, which are logically equivalent to the simplified decision tree ! Furthermore, this post defends the view that scoring systems have an intrinsic way (their weights) to highlight the factors that plays an important role in the algorithm. Decision trees does not have this property, although they have other strengths. The main point is that all algorithm builders2 should systematically think about the best way to present their work depending on the problem they address and the intended use.

The official patient questionnaire and decision tree

The Institut Pasteur (a private non-profit research foundation on diseases) and AP-HP (a world-renowned university hospital, composed by 39 hospitals) released an official tool to help patient orientation to relevant medical services:

  • a 22-item questionnaire for patients
  • an decision tree algorithm to process questionnaire answers into recommendations
  • an exact wording for the patient recommendations.

You will find the main implementation here, which has been promoted by the official Department of Health Covid-19 portal, under the heading “Do I have the coronavirus?". All documentation is in French on a dedicated website (more details here for the curious).

On the 30th of March 2020 version, there are the three types of questions: symptoms (fever, cough, diarrhea, anosmia, sore throat or muscle aches), risk factors (age, body mass index, pregnancy status, chronic conditions and current medication) and severity factors (major or minor one).

Then, there are eight possible official patient recommendations, but some of these recommendations are very similar and can be grouped. In the next section, I present a possible simplification of the orientation decision tree.

A simplified patient orientation decision tree

From the official decision tree, 2 simplifications are made:

  1. The official decision tree only considers people over 15 years of age. Instead of explicitly using a branch (denoted END1) to test for this case, I have removed it. No loss of information though, as one can add a note that explains that the new simplified tree applies only to those over 15 years of age.
  2. As mentioned previously, some recommendations (originally denoted by END2, END3, …, END8) are very similar. In the simplified decision tree version, they are grouped into three outcomes:
    • No action required (END2, END8)
    • Schedule a tele-consultation (END3, END4, END6, END7)
    • Call emergency services (END5)

Hereafter, I take the simplified decision tree as a reference. I will present an equivalent point-based scoring system which gives the same recommendation for every possible case. The motivation for a scoring system is its ability to quickly capture the most important factors of an algorithm (see final section for more details).

An equivalent point-based scoring system

First, a few words about terminology. Scoring system means that a decision variable (the score) is built by adding, subtracting or multiplying a few meaningful numbers3. Point-based insists on the fact that i. the score involves integer numbers and ii. thresholds are used to make decisions from the score.

Point-based scoring system are largely used in medical applications. For example, CHA2DS2VASc4 is used on a daily basis in cardiology departments as a decision-aid tool to prescribe anticoagulants to mitigate the stroke risk for patients with atrial fibrillation. It is a sum of 8 contributions (0pt, 1pt or 2pt) with indicative thresholds to separate low, medium and high risk patients.

Going back to the Covid-19 patient orientation problem, here is a proposal of a point-based scoring system, equivalent with the simplified decision tree:

As a potential patient, summing points and comparing it to the decision thresholds leads to a personal recommendation. In the right column of the figure above, age (53y), risk factors (0) and minor severity factors (0) results in 6 points. Presence of cough adds another 2 and anosmia increase the final score by 1. Then, the patient deduces its recommended action (schedule a tele-consultation).

Decision tree vs Scoring system: a comparative analysis

This section is a very brief discussion about merits of scoring systems compared to decision trees. Note that both algorithms are largely used: for example, Lip et al. presented both a scoring system and a clinical flowchart (an action-oriented decision tree) in their 2010 paper4 (Table 2 and Figure 1) introducing CHA2DS2VASc. Applied to machine learning, both trees and scoring systems are the building blocks of hardly any interpretable predictive model. In his 2019 interpretable machine learning book, Molnar common presents interpretable models, which almost5 all fall into the scoring system family6, tree family7(similar to rule-based models) or both8.

Coming back to the simplified Covid-19 orientation problem, there is not a clear winner between the decision trees and the scoring systems. They of course have both strengths and weaknesses, detailed below. However, the point of this post is to insist on the value of having a scoring system formulation.

The table below presents a synthesis of a comparative analysis, illustrated by Covid-19 patient story and factor importance discussion. The comparative analysis addresses the different phases of an algorithm life: Build, Implementation and the User perspective during the “Run”. The latter refers to the patient’s ability to understand his/her situation (Local importance understanding) and to understand the main decision drivers of the whole algorithm (Global importance understanding).

Easiness of design by experts + DTree processes cases by cases, successively and exhaustively, while SSys do not.
Implement Technical Both DTree and SSys can be easily programmed.
Functional Both DTree and SSys allow to potentially take some decisions before having collected all factors.

Patient 1 story: Let’s assume the patient is a 55-year-old woman who has lost her sense of taste and smell (anosmia9). She has no risk factor, nor severity factor and no other symptom. Her Covid-19 orientation score is 7, 2 points away from crossing the tele-consultation threshold. She is able to rapidly conclude that if she gets an other listed symptom different from sore throat/muscle aches (which would add only 1 point), she should schedule a tele-consultation. This insight could be derived from the simplified decision tree but in a less straightforward manner. Scoring system is the winner here!

Fever and cough importance discussion: Patients with both fever and cough should have a tele-consultation! This fever/cough interaction is one core principle of the algorithm. Identify it from the simplified decision tree is straightforward. With the scoring system, the interaction is explicitly written (4 more points if fever and cough together), but concluding that it implies a tele-consultation requires some more steps. It is only by summing 3 terms (fever, cough and the interaction term) that one can make the conclusion. A victory for the decision tree!

Weights discussion: Looking at the scoring system weights gives a quick way to assess the importance of factors. The highest weights are +23, +8, +6 and +4. The major severity factor (+23) immediately stands out as implying the emergency outcome. Then risk (+8) and minor severity (+6) factors should be considered very important but treated together (as there weights cannot add up to +14). Finally, the fever and cough interaction term (+4) raises awareness on the fact that having both fever and cough leads to a total +9 points. As a conclusion, looking at scoring system highest weights is a quick method to reliably assess the important factors, at the core of the algorithm. This method has no simple equivalent for decision trees, as detailed in the next discussion.

Top-vs-bottom node importance discussion: In a decision tree, a natural factor order emerges from the tree structure: their “relative altitude”10. Going further, one could think that there is a straightforward relationship between the “relative importance” of two factors and their “relative altitude”10 in the tree. Let us take an example from the Covid-19 tree and see what can be concluded. First, altitude does not allow to compare factors properly (fever being above and below cough at the same time). Moreover, there is no simple way to account for factors, like anosmia and sore throats, that are “diluted” in “OR” logical conditions. As a consequence, decision trees lack of a measure of importance of factor whereas scoring systems intrinsically bring their own.

Key messages

I hope that this post has:

  • enlightened your understanding of the French Covid-19 orientation algorithm.
  • convinced you that decision trees have their strengths, notably being easier to build by experts.
  • made you enthusiastic about point-based scoring systems and the measure of importance that their weights bring, both for local exploration around a particular user and for a global understanding of the algorithm.
  • made you curious about how we could compute a rigorous importance measure (see my future post on Shapley Values).
  • questioned you on how I obtained the weights of the scoring system (see my future post on ordinal regression).
  • made you, algorithm builders and machine learning practitioners, think about how you could better pick rule-based vs additive explanation methods (more on this topic later).
